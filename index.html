Academic and Professional Interests

My work is at the intersection of machine learning, neuroscience, and AI safety. I develop deep learning models to analyze neural and speech data, focusing on aligning embeddings in artificial neural networks with time-resolved recordings of neural activity to improve our understanding of how the brain processes auditory and language information

I create and share tutorials on core concepts in computational biology and computer science, making my work accessible within the broader research community. With research foundations in computational neuroscience, my current focus is in AI safety, model evaluations, and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).

My goal is to develop AI systems that are interpretable, reliable, and aligned to promote human values and scientific progress. I view these twin objectives as intrinsically connected and mutually reinforcing.

Moore's Law of Morality

Research and Career Focus

I am driven by the challenge of aligning AI systems with human values and user objectives.

More specifically, I am developing techniques for eliciting preferences from both models and users, which I see as critical for both the safe and ethical deployment of AI, as well as optimizing performance in real-world applications.

I have developed a model evaluation framework for eliciting and evaluating model preferences in integrations with cloud service providers (GCP, AWS, Azure, etc.).

You can find the CloudPref evaluation framework on my GitHub repository here:

Cloud Service Provider Preferences: Evaluating LLM Integrations.

My background in policy and international relations complements my technical work, allowing me to evaluate not only model capabilities, but also the societal impact once deployed.

Research Experience

Stanford University, Laboratory of Speech Neuroscience

Research Scientist (2023–Present)

  • Investigated neural mechanisms of speech processing using stereotactic electroencephalography (sEEG) recordings, focusing on the alignment between acoustic signals and higher-order language representations.
  • Applied decoding models to separate neural populations encoding auditory and language features, contributing to presurgical mapping and brain-computer interface research.
  • Explored the relationship between embeddings in artificial neural networks (e.g., wav2vec2.0, GPT-2) and neural responses across cortical and sub-cortical sites.
  • Developed lab protocols for data acquisition, preprocessing, and analysis, ensuring streamlined research workflows.

If you are a researcher in the field, feel free to review my project and code here:

Stereotactic Electroencephalography and Speech Processing.

New York University, Department of Psychology

Graduate Researcher (2021–2023)

  • Conducted research in the Poeppel Lab and the Max Planck NYU Center for Language, Music, and Emotion, focusing on auditory and language processing.
  • Designed and executed behavioral and MEG studies to identify individuals with absolute pitch.
  • Used magnetoencephalography (MEG) to measure audiovisual interference in a Stroop task, probing automatic label retrieval in individuals with absolute pitch.

For more details, you can review slides from my project:

Absolute Pitch: Behavioral and MEG Experiments

Cornell University, Department of Government

  • Teaching assistant for a course on the science of social behavior, assisting with curriculum development and leading discussions.
